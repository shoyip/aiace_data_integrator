[Discontinued] Movement Between Tiles v1


Questions the maps help answer


  • How much is movement disrupted by a crisis event?
  • Where are populations going after they evacuate due to a disaster?
  • Where should services be positioned in the aftermath of a disaster?

What you can do with these maps


  • Identify both origin and destination tiles with abnormal amounts of population movement
  • Identify areas heavily impacted by disaster
  • Understand dynamics over time and estimate how many people are impacted

Who this dataset counts

These maps show how many Facebook app users (who have opted to share their Location History data) have moved from tile A to tile B between 8-hour windows while preserving the users’ privacy. By aggregating Location History data, we are able to show a smoothed representation of how many Location History users have moved from one tile to another for each time period.

Note that this dataset is showing only a subset of the total population in an area, specifically only those Facebook app users who have opted in to sharing their device’s Location History data with the Facebook app. This is not the total population moving.

These counts are also compared to precrisis movement levels between the 2 tiles. This can point to areas that are more impacted by a crisis, describing how evacuations are occurring or how regular movement patterns have been disrupted.


When these maps are generated

The Facebook app’s Safety Check feature triggers creation of the maps, which cover only the areas affected by a crisis event. This dataset is generated for each area and time experiencing a crisis. Partners are also welcome to request dataset generation.


Movement Between Tiles vs. other mobility maps

Movement Between Tiles is sometimes called Facebook Movement Maps (Tile Version) or Movement Vectors because it shows the count of people moving from one tile to another. A very similar dataset is called Movement Between Administrative Regions. Several additional datasets describe people’s mobility, including Movement Range Maps and Colocation Maps. The naming of this dataset causes some confusion.


Common mistakes to avoid with this dataset

When new users see the name Movement Maps, they think this is the dataset they want to use because they are interested in human mobility. This is unfortunate because the visualization and interpretation of Facebook Population (Tile Version) is a much more beginner-friendly dataset to view.

The inclination to dive first into movement maps can lead partners to a common mistake. They try to add up all the inbound and outbound movement for a single tile in order to calculate a net flux for the tile of interest. This is not advised, as any movement vector with fewer than 10 users is excluded to protect privacy. In addition, the net flux is actually provided by the Facebook Population (Tile Version) map, as adding up all the inbound and outbound movement leaves a single count of total users.


Understanding vector values

Vector values are significantly more complex to comprehend and visualize than simple population counts per tile. A vector has a direction defined by the starting and ending tile, and metrics comparing crisis to baseline can have both positive and negative values. So for example the vector from tile A to tile B can be a value of +X, but that does not mean that the vector from tile B to tile A is -X.

An easy way to understand the nature of this dataset is to think of commuting. These maps can tell you how many people went from tile A in San Francisco to tile B in Menlo Park this morning and whether that is more or fewer than a normal day before the crisis. They can also tell you how many people went from tile B in Menlo Park to tile A in San Francisco during the same time period. Of course the magnitude of those counts will be very different.


Calculating movement between tiles

The Movement Between Tiles data represents the most common location (Bing tile) of the user within the first time window, and then the most common location within the second time window. This defines the starting and ending of the vector. The vector time is then assigned to the starting time (in UTC) in the second window.

So for example an individual’s start coordinates for time window A are the tile center for their modal Bing tile (the most common Bing tile among all their locations in the time period). The end coordinates are obtained the same way, but for time window B, which is 8 hours later. The user’s start and end coordinates are then used to assign them to a vector. Then the user’s start and end coordinates are averaged along with those of everyone else assigned to that vector to yield the value surfaced in the dataset.


People who remain within a single tile

The Movement Between Tiles data also shows people who remained within a single tile, which is a common point of confusion. Data is provided for the number of people who “moved” from tile A to tile A between 8-hour windows.


Data standards

Population sample: Movement Between Tiles is derived from Facebook mobile app users who have enabled Location History sharing.

Minimum spatial aggregation: We surface the Movement Between Tiles counts on the smallest tile size that can be completed within an 8-hour update frequency, starting with the minimum tile size allowed for privacy protections, Bing tile level 16. This is equivalent to roughly 600 meters on a side near the equator or the size of 2 city blocks. If we see that our calculations will not finish within the 8-hour update window, we move to the next-largest Bing tile until we find a size that will not delay the results. The complexity for this calculation is a function of the overall boundary rectangle size (which determines the number of tiles inside it) and the number of unique Location History users within the boundary rectangle. This ensures that the version of the datasets available is the most granular over space and time allowed by our privacy protections.

Note: We cannot dynamically alter the tile size or time window for this metric given the complexity of our calculations.

Because Movement Between Tiles is a cross product of all tiles, the computation for it is much more complex than the Facebook Population (Tile Level) dataset, so these datasets will often be surfaced at different tile sizes.

Temporal aggregation: Movement Between Tiles is aggregated over 2 adjacent 8-hour time windows. The first (start) 8-hour time segment ends and the second (end) 8-hour window begins at the value shown in date/time.

Update frequency and dataset start date: Dataset generation is triggered by either a Safety Check on the Facebook platform or a request to the Data for Good at Meta program. By default the dataset is updated every 8 hours for 14 days, but additional time can be requested. The dataset is accessible for 90 days after the final update day and then is discarded.

Minimum counts: If either the baseline or users during crisis time count is less than a threshold value of 10 users, then both are dropped.

Crossing international borders: If the starting quadkey and ending quadkey represent different countries, then we drop that vector from the dataset as this would indicate crossing an international border.


Codebook

These take the form human-friendly metric name (metric name in csv).

Geometry (geometry): Geometric representation (like an arrow) of a movement vector plotted on a map. The values within the common linestring function notation denote the starting and ending latitudinal and longitudinal points of the arrow. These are the average of all the starting and ending locations of all users represented in the vector and thus will be different than the centroid latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates provided in starting latitude, starting longitude, ending latitude and ending longitude.

Date/Time (date_time): The time period represented by the data reporting. This value is the end time (in Coordinated Universal Time, commonly known as UTC) of the first 8-hour segment and thus also the start time of the second 8-hour segment that defines the vector.

Starting polygon ID (start_polygon_ID): The unique identifier for the administrative polygon containing the starting movement vector. The value is assigned according to the latitudinal and longitudinal position of the tile centroid against this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Starting polygon name (start_polygon_name): The human-readable name for the administrative polygon containing the starting tile. The value is assigned according to the latitudinal and longitudinal position of the tile centroid against this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Ending polygon ID (end_polygon_ID): The unique identifier for the administrative polygon containing the ending tile. The value is assigned according to this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Ending polygon name (end_polygon_name): The human-readable name for the administrative polygon containing the ending tile. The value is assigned according to the latitudinal and longitudinal position of the tile centroid against this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Length in kilometers (length_km): The distance in kilometers between the starting and ending latitude and longitude points that define the vector

Tile size (tile_size): The Bing tile level used to define the starting and ending tiles of the vector. The smallest tile size allowed is level 16, which is approximately 0.6 kilometers on a side at the equator. The larger the map area, the more likely that coarser or larger tiles will be used in order to maintain the 8-hour update frequency.

Country (country): The 2-letter abbreviation (ISO alpha-2 code) for the starting and ending country (any vector where the start and end country are different is excluded). The country value is assigned according to this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining country boundaries. A value of \N indicates that no country code is available for the administrative region, usually the result of being over a body of water such as the ocean.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Level (level): Administrative level of the starting and ending polygons. Most common are level 3 for public health crises and level 4 for natural disasters.
Using the United States as an example, level 1 is US, level 2 is Florida, level 3 is Dade County and level 4 is Miami.

People during crisis (n_crisis): The number of people:

  • Who have opted in to sharing Location History data with the Facebook app and
  • Whose most common tile during the 8-hour segment ending at date/time was starting quadkey (start_quadkey) and
  • Whose most common tile during the 8-hour segment beginning at date/time was ending quadkey (end_quadkey)

If the same person appeared at multiple tiles in either time interval, we only count their most frequent tile, choosing the latest of their most frequent tiles in the event of a tie.

To protect privacy, we use a number of techniques to obfuscate the exact count. These are all typical of methods used to protect privacy in geospatial population datasets like the census.

  • Random noise: We add a small amount of random noise is added to the crisis count to ensure that it's impossible to ascertain precise, true counts for sparsely populated locations.
  • Dropping small counts: We drop locations with small counts from the final datasets. If either the baseline or crisis time count is less than a threshold value of 10, we drop both.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Baseline (n_baseline): Average number of users:

  • Who have opted in to sharing Location History data with the Facebook app and
  • Whose most common tile during the 8-hour segment ending at date/time (date_time) was starting quadkey (start_quadkey) and
  • Whose most common tile during the 8-hour segment beginning at date/time (date_time) was ending quadkey (end_quadkey)

The baseline period is the 45 days going back from the day the maps were started (you can see this by looking at the day the first data is generated). The baseline value is segmented by the day of the week and the time window for the metric. Thus the baseline value for 00:00 UTC on a Tuesday is the mean value (outliers are winsorized, not removed) across all the 00:00 UTC plus Tuesdays in the 45 days prior to when the maps first generated. This means the baseline is a set period of days prior to day zero on the maps, not a rolling window. So for example each region in the dataset will have 7 days of 3 8-hour windows (00:00 UTC, 08:00 UTC and 16:00 UTC) for a total of 21 unique baseline values across the dataset.

If the same person appeared at multiple tiles in a time interval, we only count their most frequent tile, choosing the latest of their most frequent tiles in the event of a tie. If this number is below 10, the value will appear as \N as this is below the level required for privacy protection.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Note: We can’t backfill this dataset to previous dates because the baseline metric calculation already includes the entire time period that Meta retains Facebook Location History data (in this case, 90 days). From the moment these maps are kicked off, they are already using all the past data that we have available to create the dataset.

Difference between baseline and crisis (n_difference): The number of users who have opted to share their Location History data with the Facebook mobile app that are present during the crisis minus the average number of users present during the baseline period

Percent change (percent_change): Percent change in Facebook Location History user population counts along the vector defined by the starting quadkey (start_quadkey) and ending quadkey (end_quadkey), calculated by dividing the difference by the baseline (n_baseline) (plus a small value, usually 1).
More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Z-score (z_score): The number of standard deviations the people during crisis (n_crisis) value is above or below the baseline (n_baseline)

The z-score (sometimes called the standard score) highlights the areas with the most significant differences between what is observed during the crisis and what is typically seen during the baseline. Our recommended metric, it prevents the measured change from being dominated by dense areas (which tends to be the case when using only difference between baseline and crisis) or sparse rural areas (which tends to be the case when using percent change). The calculated z-score is clipped to be a value between -4 and 4.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Starting latitude (start_lat): Latitude coordinate of the center of the Bing tile grid cell for the origin or start of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Starting longitude (start_lon): Longitude coordinate of the center of the Bing tile grid cell for the origin or start of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Ending latitude (end_lat): Latitude coordinate for the center of the Bing tile that represents the destination or end of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Ending longitude (end_lon): Longitude coordinate of the center of the Bing tile that represents the destination or end of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Starting quadkey (start_quadkey): The unique identifier for the grid cell defining the start of the vector in the Bing tile system. Each quadkey uniquely identifies a single tile at a particular level of detail. Quadkeys provide a one-dimensional index key that usually preserves the proximity of tiles in latitudinal and longitudinal space. In other words, 2 tiles that have nearby latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates usually have quadkeys that are relatively close together. Counting the number of characters in a quadkey string is a quick way to determine the tile’s spatial granularity.
Note: Leading zeros are important and must be preserved in order to properly identify the tile. Although the values appear numeric, we recommend converting them into a string. Common spreadsheet tools like Excel and Google Sheets will not do this by default.

Ending quadkey (end_quadkey): The unique identifier for the grid cell that represents the destination or end of the movement vector in the Bing tile system.

Each quadkey uniquely identifies a single tile at a particular level of detail. Quadkeys provide a one-dimensional index key that usually preserves the proximity of tiles in latitudinal and longitudinal space. In other words, 2 tiles that have nearby latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates usually have quadkeys that are relatively close together. Counting the number of characters in a quadkey string is a quick way to determine the tile’s spatial granularity.
