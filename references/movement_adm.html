[Discontinued] Movement Between Administrative Regions v1


Questions the maps help answer


  • How much is a crisis event disrupting movement or commuting levels?
  • Where are populations going after they evacuate due to a disaster?
  • Where should services be positioned in the aftermath of a disaster?

What you can do with these maps


  • Identify both origin and destination regions with abnormal amounts of population movement.
  • Identify areas heavily impacted by disaster.
  • Understand dynamics over time and estimate how many people are impacted.

Who this dataset counts

These maps show how many Facebook app users (who have opted to share their location history data) have moved from administrative region A to administrative region B between 8-hour time windows while preserving the users’ privacy. By aggregating location history data, we are able to show a smoothed representation of how many people have moved from one region to another for each time period.

Note that this dataset is showing only a subset of the total population in an area, specifically only those Facebook app users who have opted in to sharing their device’s location history data with the Facebook app. This is not the total population moving.

These counts are also compared to precrisis movement levels between the 2 regions. This can point to areas that are more impacted by a crisis, describing how evacuations are occurring or how regular movement patterns have been disrupted.


When these maps are generated

The Facebook app’s Safety Check feature triggers creation of the maps, which cover only the areas affected by a crisis event. This dataset is generated for each area and time experiencing a crisis. Partners are also welcome to request dataset generation.


Movement Between Administrative Regions vs. other mobility maps

Movement Between Administrative Regions is sometimes called Facebook Movement Maps (Administrative Regions) or Movement Vectors because it shows the count of people moving from one region to another. A very similar dataset is called Movement Between Tiles. Several additional datasets describe people’s mobility, including Movement Range Maps and Colocation Maps. The naming of this dataset causes some confusion.


Understanding vector values

Vector values are significantly more complex to comprehend and visualize than simple population counts per tile or per region. A vector has a direction defined by the starting and ending region, and metrics comparing crisis to baseline can have both positive and negative values. So for example the vector from region A to region B can be a value of +X, but that does not mean that the vector from region B to region A is -X.

An easy way to understand the nature of this dataset is to think of commuting. These maps can tell you how many people went from region A in San Francisco to region B in Menlo Park this morning and whether that is more or less than a normal day before the crisis. They can also tell you how many people went from region B in Menlo Park to region A in San Francisco during the same time period. Of course the magnitude of those counts will be very different.


Calculating movement between administrative regions

The Movement Between Administrative Regions data represents the most common location (in this case administrative region) of the user within the first time window and then the most common location within the second time window. This defines the starting and ending of the vector. The vector time is then assigned to the starting time (in UTC) in the second window.

So for example an individual’s start coordinates for time window A are the administrative region center for their modal administrative region (the most common region among all their locations in the time period). The end coordinates are obtained the same way, but for time window B, which is 8 hours later. The person’s start and end coordinates are then used to assign them to a vector. Then their start and end coordinates are averaged along with those of everyone else assigned to that vector to yield the value surfaced in the dataset.


People who remain within a single region

The Movement Between Administrative Regions data also shows Facebook users who remained within a single region, which is a common point of confusion. Data is provided for the number of users who “moved” from region A to region A between 8-hour windows.


Data standards

Population sample: Movement Between Administrative Regions counts the number of Facebook mobile app users who have enabled location history sharing.

Minimum spatial aggregation: We surface the Movement Between Administrative Regions counts on the smallest administrative boundary level that can be completed within its update frequency time of 8 hours, starting with the minimum level 4. This is equivalent to the boundary of a US neighborhood or district. If we see that our calculations will not finish within the 8-hour update window, we move to the next-largest level until we find a size that will not delay the results. The complexity for this calculation is a function of the overall boundary rectangle size (which determines the number of subregions inside it) and the number of unique location history users within the boundary rectangle. This ensures that the version of the datasets available are the most granular over space and time allowed by our privacy protections.

Note: We cannot dynamically alter the level or time window size used for this metric given the complexity of our calculations.

Temporal aggregation: Movement Between Administrative Regions is aggregated over 2 adjacent 8-hour time windows. The first (start) 8-hour time segment ends and the second (end) 8-hour window begins at the value shown in date/time. Dataset generation is triggered by either a Safety Check on the Facebook platform or a request to the Data for Good at Meta program. By default the dataset is updated every 8 hours for 14 days, but additional time can be requested. The dataset is accessible for 90 days after the final update day and then is discarded.

Minimum counts: If either the baseline or users during crisis time count is less than a threshold value of 10 users, then both are dropped.

Crossing international borders: If the starting quadkey and ending quadkey represent different countries, then we drop that vector from the dataset as this would indicate crossing an international border.


Codebook

These take the form human-friendly metric name (metric name in csv).

Geometry (GEOMETRY): Geometric representation (like an arrow) of a movement vector plotted on a map. The values within the common linestring function notation denote the starting and ending latitudinal and longitudinal points of the arrow. These are the average of all the starting and ending locations of all users represented in the vector and thus will be different than the centroid latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates provided in starting latitude, starting longitude, ending latitude and ending longitude.

Date/Time: The time period represented by the data reporting. This value is the end time (in Coordinated Universal Time, commonly known as UTC) of the first 8-hour segment and thus also the start time of the second 8-hour segment that defines the vector.

Starting polygon ID (start_polygon_ID): The unique identifier for the administrative polygon containing the starting movement vector. The value is assigned according to the latitudinal and longitudinal position of the tile centroid against this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Starting polygon name (start_polygon_name): The human-readable name for the administrative polygon containing the starting tile. The value is assigned according to the latitudinal and longitudinal position of the tile centroid against this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Ending polygon ID (end_polygon_ID): The unique identifier for the administrative polygon containing the ending administrative region. The value is assigned according to this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.

Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly-licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Ending polygon name (end_polygon_name): The human-readable name for the administrative polygon containing the ending of the movement vector. The value is assigned according to this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining administrative boundaries, either at admin level 3 or admin level 4.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Length in kilometers (length_km): The distance in kilometers between the starting and ending latitude and longitude points that define the vector

Country (country): The 2-letter abbreviation (ISO alpha-2 code) for the starting and ending country (any vector where the start and end country are different is excluded). The country value is assigned according to this commercially purchased set of polygon shapes defining country boundaries. A value of \N indicates that no country code is available for the administrative region, usually the result of being over a body of water such as the ocean.
Note: In the future we plan to migrate from using this commercial polygon set to more openly licensed alternatives such as GADM.

Level: Administrative level of the starting and ending polygons. Most common are level 3 for public health crises and level 4 for natural disasters.
Using the United States as an example, level 1 is US, level 2 is Florida, level 3 is Dade County and level 4 is Miami.

People during crisis (n_crisis): The number of people:

  • Who have opted in to sharing Location History data with the Facebook app and
  • Whose most common region during the 8-hour segment ending at date/time was starting polygon name and
  • Whose most common region during the 8-hour segment beginning at date/time was ending polygon name

If the same person appeared at multiple regions in either time interval, we only count their most frequent region, choosing the latest of their most frequent regions in the event of a tie.

To protect privacy, we use a number of techniques to obfuscate the exact count. These are all typical of methods used to protect privacy in geospatial population datasets like the census:

  • Random noise: We add a small amount of random noise is added to the crisis count to ensure that it's impossible to ascertain precise, true counts for sparsely populated locations.
  • Dropping small counts: We drop locations with small counts from the final datasets. If either the baseline or crisis time count is less than a threshold value of 10, we drop both.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Baseline (n_baseline): The average number of people:

  • Who have opted in to sharing location history data with the Facebook app and
  • Whose most common region during the 8-hour segment ending at date/time was starting polygon name and
  • Whose most common region during the 8-hour segment beginning at date/time was ending polygon name

The baseline period is the 45 days going back from the day the maps were started (you can see this by looking at the day the first data is generated). The baseline value is segmented by the day of the week and the time window for the metric. Thus the baseline value for 00:00 UTC on a Tuesday is the mean value (outliers are winsorized, not removed) across all the 00:00 UTC plus Tuesdays in the 45 days prior to when the maps first generated. This means the baseline is a set period of days prior to day zero on the maps, not a rolling window. So for example each region in the dataset will have 7 days of 3 8-hour windows (00:00 UTC, 08:00 UTC and 16:00 UTC) for a total of 21 unique baseline values across the dataset.

If the same person appeared at multiple regions in a time interval, we only count their most frequent region, choosing the latest of their most frequent regions in the event of a tie. If this number is below 10, the value will appear as \N as this is below the level required for privacy protection.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Note: We can’t backfill this dataset to previous dates because the baseline metric calculation already includes the entire time period that Meta retains Facebook location history data (in this case, 90 days). From the moment these maps are kicked off, they are already using all the past data that we have available to create the dataset.

Difference between baseline and crisis (n_difference): The number of people present during the crisis minus the average number of people present during the baseline period

Percent change (percent_change): Percent change in Facebook (Location History) user population counts along the vector defined by the starting polygon name and ending polygon name, calculated by dividing the difference by the baseline (plus a small value, usually 1).
More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Z-score (z_score): The number of standard deviations the people during crisis value is above or below the baseline.

The z-score (sometimes also called the standard score) highlights the areas with the most significant differences between what is being observed during the crisis and what is typically seen during the baseline. Our recommended metric, it prevents the measured change from being dominated by dense area (which tends to be the case when using only difference between baseline and crisis) or sparse rural areas (which tends to be the case when using percent change). The calculated z-score is clipped to be a value between -4 and 4.

More details are provided in this peer-reviewed publication: https://research.fb.com/publications/facebook-disaster-maps-aggregate-insights-for-crisis-response-recovery/

Starting latitude (start_lat): Latitude coordinate of the center of the administrative polygon for the origin or start of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Starting longitude (start_lon): Longitude coordinate of the center of the administrative polygon for the origin or start of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Ending latitude (end_lat): Latitude coordinate of the center of the administrative region polygon for the destination or end of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.

Ending longitude (end_lon): Longitude coordinate of the center of the administrative region polygon for the destination or end of the movement vector. We use the Presto geospatial library to generate this field.
